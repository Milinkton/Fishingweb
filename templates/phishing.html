What is phishing, its history, how to recognize
such sites and how to protect yourself.

Let's turn to the concept of phishing:

Phishing is a set of methods to deceive a user and force him to disclose his password, credit card number and other confidential information. Most often, attackers impersonate representatives of well-known organizations in emails or phone calls.

Phishing history:

The origin of the term "phishing" is quite easy to trace. A phishing attack is largely similar to ordinary fishing. First you need to get a bait that can mislead the victim, and then throw a fishing rod and wait for the "fish" to bite. In the 1970s, a subculture was formed, representatives of which used a number of low tech methods to hack telephone networks. These early hackers were called "phreaks", which is a combination of the words "phone" and "freak". At that time, the number of computers connected to the network was small, so the purpose of fracking was to make free international calls or calls to numbers not included in phone books. The use of this term begins in the mid-1990s, and its first mention is attributed to the infamous spammer and hacker Khan C Smith. Interesting fact! The first case of public mention of the term "phishing" has been preserved on the Internet. With the advent of the 2000s, phishing fraudsters began to pay attention to the vulnerabilities of electronic payment systems. Clients of banks and payment systems began to become increasingly victims of phishing, and in some cases - as the subsequent investigation showed - the attackers even managed not only to accurately identify their victims, but also to find out which bank they used. Cybercriminals registered dozens of domains that so elegantly imitated resources such as eBay and PayPal that many not too attentive users simply did not notice the substitution. PayPal customers received phishing emails containing links to the front website asking them to update their credit card number and other personal data. 1 In the mid-2000s, phishing malware could be ordered on a turnkey basis on the black market. At the same time, hackers began to coordinate their actions to organize increasingly sophisticated phishing attacks. Interesting fact! In 2013, 110 million credit card records and credentials belonging to Target retail chain customers were stolen.

Types of phishing attacks:

Targeted phishing. In this way, attackers attack a specific person or organization, often using specially selected content that they think will have the greatest impact on the victim. To carry out such an attack, careful preparation is required to find out names, positions, email addresses and other related information. Hackers turn upside down the entire Internet, comparing this information with all available information about the victim's job relationship: for example, they are interested in the names of colleagues and their responsibilities in the relevant organization. Having received all this data, the attackers make a plausible letter. Targeted phishing poses a significant danger to business and the state, as it can lead to significant losses. Interesting fact! A verbose phishing letter from an unknown person calling himself a Nigerian prince is one of the earliest and longest manifestations of such attacks. Clone phishing. This type of attack assumes that attackers copy a previously delivered legal message that contains a link or attachment. The fraudster then changes links or attached files to malicious objects ident as real ones. Unsuspecting users click on the link or open the attachment, which is often enough for hackers to seize control of the computer. After that, attackers can disguise themselves as reliable senders and send similar emails on behalf of the victim to other users within the same organization. 2 A story from life! In 2016, the British website Anorak reported that its editorial office received an email from a certain Dr. Bakare Tunde, who introduced himself as a space project manager working at the Nigerian National Space Research Agency. Dr. Tunde claimed that his cousin, aviation major Abak Tunde, has been on the old Soviet space station for more than 25 years. But for only $3 million, the managers of Roscosmos Corporation agreed to organize a flight of the manned ship and return the Nigerian cosmonaut to Earth. The recipient of such a letter was required to "just" provide his bank account details so that Nigerian specialists could transfer the necessary amount to their Russian colleagues. As a reward, Dr. Tunde promised to pay $600,000. Telephone phishing.
Phishing attacks can occur using a regular phone: a fraudster calls his victim and introduces himself as an employee of a local bank, police or tax authority. Then he intimidates the victim by reporting a problem and insisting that it must be solved immediately, and to do this, you need to inform him with his bank account details or pay a fine. Usually scammers require money to be transferred by bank transfer or with a prepaid card so that it cannot be tracked. SMS phishing is an evil twin brother of rishing who performs the same fraudulent actions, but only via SMS messages.

Here are some of the most important rules that will help you not to fall for the hook of scammers:

• Do not open emails from unfamiliar senders.

• Click on the link inside the email only if you know exactly where it leads.

• After receiving an email from a dubious sender, click on the attached link manually - enter the address of the legal website in the browser address bar using the keyboard, so you will provide yourself with another level of security.

• Check the digital certificates of websites.

• If you are asked to disclose confidential data, make sure that the URL of the web page starts with "HTTPS", not just "HTTP". The letter "S" means "secure", that is, the connection with such an address is secure. However, this does not guarantee that the website is legal, but most legitimate websites use HTTPS because of its greater security. At the same time, even legitimate websites using the HTTP protocol are vulnerable to hacker attacks.

• If you suspect that the received email was sent by a fraudster, enter the sender's name or an excerpt of the text of the letter in the search engine - and you will see if there are any phishing attacks associated with this letter.

• Move the mouse cursor over the links to make sure they are reliable. Be vigilant and you will never get into such situations!
